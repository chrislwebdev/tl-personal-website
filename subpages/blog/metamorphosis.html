Observations of a novice designer in 2023; Metamorphosis.

This article is about the perspective of a novice product designer in 2023. One and a half years ago, when I first heard about user experience design, it felt like the missing piece in this highly industrialized and digitized world was found. We are all surrounded by thousands of physical and digital products, and a very small minority of them function to a high standard. Let's be honest, products mostly disappoint us. I have always been one of those people who complains non-stop about dysfunctional packaging and products, particularly how and why nobody puts the necessary effort and thought into the design before they were produced. So, when I heard about UX design, it was music to my ears. Briefly, I felt sad not to choose a profession like that, and then later thought I need a challenge like that, as well as a serious career change, in my late 30s.

I know it's a cliché, but I do believe that design is intelligence made visual, as in somebody fully comprehended the purpose of the product, put thought into each step, and designed it in a way in which it functions smoothly. It's as if the designer is saying, “Hello, I'm a good designer, or, if you prefer, the solution bringer.”

Being a self-taught designer

As a new learner, it was hard to evaluate the merit of different learning resources, be it a mentor or a course. The nature of self-taught learning is that you are very likely to lose time and energy, meaning failing and learning is a big part of the process. Combined with the lack of feedback, a good learning road map to lead you to success, and the sheer confusion of numerous input from various design influencers, it might make becoming a designer sound like a hellish journey. Thankfully, it's not all doom and gloom. On the upside, there are absolutely no time restrictions, you go at your own pace, and you can choose whatever fits your learning style.

2023 Sector Conditions

I must admit that I feel very unlucky to have entered the industry at the stage it is currently at. Looking back, the way in which the internet and mobiles entered our lives seemed effortless. With direct access to people like never before, companies capitalized on the opportunity, central to which was their design decisions. Suddenly, those companies could earn millions with magic hat tricks – design practices that were newly discovered and proved to be highly effective for user-centered design, resulting in massive profits and making UX a new golden attraction - but the lack of experienced designer resulted in a boom of boot camps, who weren't necessarily interested in producing the new wave of talented UX designers, instead seeking quick profits themselves. Over the course of a few years, the lack of talent caused the image of UX designers to quickly deteriorate.

Still, companies were as expectant as ever, and the pandemic almost sent them giddy, hiring like never before in a quest to ensure the profits kept flowing. If there's one thing you can guarantee about a boom, however, it's that it's followed by a bust, and that bust came in the form of AI and global economic uncertainty. This has resulted in job openings being 70% less, and now, more than ever, the industry is saturated with self-proclaimed designers.

Perhaps the current state of the industry is a blessing in disguise. I can consider myself lucky in that I get to show my true love of design, and I get more and more competent as I wait for this storm to subside. As my design maturity increases, I can patiently wait for the day to come that I can convert good ideas into good products.
